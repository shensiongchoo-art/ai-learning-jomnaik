The Art of Prompting Claude

The same question can get a mediocre answer or a brilliant one — depending on how you ask. Here's how to ask well.

Before & After — See the Difference

Technique 1: Give Claude a Role Role-Setting
❌ Vague Prompt
"Explain machine learning."
Machine learning is a subset of artificial intelligence that uses algorithms to parse data, learn from it, and make informed decisions. It includes supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning paradigms…
✅ With a Role
"You are a patient teacher explaining to someone with no technical background. Explain machine learning using a simple everyday analogy."
Imagine teaching a child to spot dogs in photos. You show them 1,000 pictures — "dog" or "not dog" — until they get good at it. Machine learning works the same way: instead of a child, it's a computer finding patterns from examples.
Why it works: Claude adapts to the role you give it. "Patient teacher for non-technical person" reshapes tone, vocabulary, and analogy choice entirely.
Technique 2: Specify the Output Format Format Control
❌ No Format
"Give me tips for a job interview."
Sure! For a job interview, preparation is key. You should research the company beforehand and make sure you understand the role. It's also important to practice your answers…
✅ Format Specified
"Give me 5 job interview tips as a numbered list. Each tip: one bold headline + one sentence explanation. Keep it under 150 words total."
1. Research the company. Spend 20 mins on their website so you can reference their work naturally.
2. Prepare your "why us" answer. Interviewers always ask — have a specific, genuine reason ready. …
Why it works: Claude is excellent at following format instructions. Numbered list, word count, structure — specify it and you get exactly that.
Technique 3: Give Context About Yourself Context
❌ No Context
"How do I ask for a raise?"
To ask for a raise, schedule a meeting with your manager, prepare your case with data on your contributions, research market rates, and choose a good time…
✅ With Context
"I'm a 28-year-old graphic designer at a small agency in Malaysia. I've been there 2 years, just led a big campaign, but I'm nervous about confrontation. How do I ask for a raise in a way that feels natural, not aggressive?"
Given your context — a small team, a recent win, and a preference for warmth over confrontation — try framing it as a conversation about growth rather than a demand. Start with: "I'd love to talk about where I'm headed here…"
Why it works: Claude uses every detail you give it. Situation, personality, cultural context, constraints — the more specific you are, the more tailored the response.
Technique 4: Ask Claude to Think Step-by-Step Reasoning
❌ Direct Question
"Should I quit my job to start a business?"
It depends on your financial situation, the strength of your idea, your risk tolerance, and whether you have a support system…
✅ Step-by-Step
"I'm thinking of quitting my job to start a business. Think through this step by step: What are the key questions I should answer first? What are the biggest risks? What would a cautious advisor say vs. an encouraging one?"
Step 1 — Key questions to answer first: Do you have 6 months of living expenses saved? Is there evidence people will pay for this? … Step 2 — Biggest risks: … Step 3 — Cautious advisor: … Encouraging advisor: …
Why it works: When you ask Claude to think step-by-step, it actually reasons more carefully. It's one of the most reliable ways to get deeper, more structured thinking.
Technique 5: Tell It What NOT to Do Constraints
❌ No Constraints
"Write a bio for my LinkedIn."
Sarah is a passionate and results-driven professional with a proven track record of delivering innovative solutions and leveraging synergies across cross-functional teams…
✅ With Constraints
"Write a LinkedIn bio for me. Do NOT use: 'passionate', 'results-driven', 'synergies', or corporate buzzwords. Write in first person, warm tone, under 80 words."
I'm a graphic designer who loves turning messy briefs into clean, memorable work. For the past 5 years I've been helping brands in Southeast Asia look as good as their ideas. Currently open to freelance projects and full-time roles.
Why it works: Claude takes "don't do X" seriously. Banning clichés, formats, or tones often produces more creative, human-sounding output than vague positive instructions.

The 5 Techniques — Quick Reference

1
Give Claude a Role
"You are a patient teacher for non-technical people…"
Start your prompt with a role. The more specific, the better. "You are a no-nonsense financial advisor" gives very different output than "you are a warm, encouraging coach."
2
Specify the Format
"Give me a numbered list. Each item: bold headline + one sentence."
Tell Claude exactly what you want: bullet list, numbered steps, table, paragraph, email format. Add length limits: "under 100 words", "exactly 3 paragraphs".
3
Give Your Context
"I'm a 28-year-old designer in Malaysia who's nervous about confrontation…"
Include: your background, the situation, your constraints, your audience. Claude uses every detail to personalise the response. More context = more relevant answer.
4
Ask for Step-by-Step Thinking
"Think through this step by step…"
Adding "think step by step" or "reason through this carefully before answering" makes Claude slow down and reason more deeply. Great for complex decisions, analysis, and planning.
5
Add Constraints (What NOT to Do)
"Do NOT use buzzwords. Do NOT exceed 150 words."
Negative constraints are powerful. Banning clichés, formats, or styles often unlocks more original output. Pair with positive direction: "Avoid X. Instead, aim for Y."

Key Facts

What to remember about prompting Claude

  • Claude responds to role — tell it who to be and it adapts tone, depth, and vocabulary
  • Claude follows format instructions precisely — numbered lists, word limits, structure all work
  • Context is gold — the more you share about yourself and your situation, the more tailored the response
  • "Think step by step" genuinely improves reasoning quality — not just a trick
  • Negative constraints often produce better results than vague positive ones — "don't use jargon" beats "keep it simple"
