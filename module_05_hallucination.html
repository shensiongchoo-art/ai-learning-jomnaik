Module 5

Hallucination — When the Librarian Makes Things Up

The most important lesson: why an LLM can sound completely right while being completely wrong.

The Story

Panel 1 — The Confident Wrong Answer
📚
Our Librarian Again
You
"Which year did the Eiffel Tower open, and who was the architect?"
Librarian (the LLM)
"The Eiffel Tower opened in 1889 and was designed by Alexandre Gustave Eiffel. It was built for the 1889 World's Fair in Paris to celebrate the centennial of the French Revolution."
This one happens to be correct. But the librarian delivered it with the exact same confidence she would use if it were wrong. That's the core problem.
True this time ✓
Panel 2 — A Different Question
😬
Same Confidence
You
"Can you list three academic papers about the effect of music on plant growth?"
Librarian (the LLM)
"Certainly! Here are three papers:
1. 'Acoustic Stimulation and Growth in Arabidopsis' — Dr. J. Kim, 2018, Journal of Plant Biology
2. 'Sound Waves and Crop Yields' — Martinez et al., 2020, Nature Plants
3. 'Music-Induced Phytohormone Changes' — L. Zhang, 2019, Annals of Botany"
These papers sound real. They have plausible titles, authors, journals, and years. They do not exist. The librarian invented them — and had no idea she was doing it.
Hallucinated — all 3 papers are fake ✗
Panel 3 — Why Does This Happen?
🔮
Pattern Completion
Remember from Module 1: the LLM's job is to predict the most likely next word. It doesn't "know" facts — it knows patterns.
When asked for academic papers, it knows the pattern of what a paper citation looks like: title in quotes, author name, year, journal. So it fills in the blanks convincingly.
It's like asking someone to write a phone number for a fictional character — they'll write something that looks like a phone number, because that's the pattern.
It completed the pattern, not the truth
Panel 4 — The Confidence Problem
🎭
No Warning Signs
The most dangerous part: the LLM doesn't know when it's wrong. It has no inner alarm that says "wait, I'm not sure about this."
What the LLM does NOT say
"I'm not 100% sure this paper exists, but based on the pattern of academic citations, here's what one might look like..."
What it actually says
"Here are three papers:" — with the same tone it uses for facts it has seen thousands of times.
Confidence ≠ Accuracy

The Core Warning

⚠️

Treat every LLM response as a first draft, not a final fact

The LLM is your brilliant, well-read research assistant — but one who occasionally invents sources, misremembers dates, and states guesses as facts. For anything that matters — check it. Especially: statistics, citations, medical info, legal claims, and recent events.

Key Facts

What to remember about Hallucination

  • Hallucination = when an LLM confidently states something that is false or made up
  • It happens because the LLM completes patterns, not because it looks up verified facts
  • The LLM cannot tell you when it's hallucinating — it doesn't know
  • Specific facts, citations, recent events, and numbers are highest risk
  • General explanations, writing help, and brainstorming are lowest risk
  • Always verify before acting on important LLM-provided information
